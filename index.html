To build architecture is to bury archaeology.

The dominant firm of a generation defines the foundation of the next. Better alternatives are foreclosed not because they are worse, but because switching costs exceed switching benefit. The market chooses a local maximum and entrenches it for a century.

Every design decision lays down a stratum. Some are deliberate; most are contingent: a full disk in 1971, a wartime wage cap, the width of a colliery wagon. The choice gets load-bearing, then permanent, then invisible. This is a comprehensive field catalog of those strata: the accidents we froze, the roads we foreclosed, and the fossils still running underneath our systems.
